MY JOURNEY

Growth, Challenges, and Learning in Computer Science

When I started AP Computer Science, I underestimated how much this course would reshape the way I think about problem-solving. What began as learning syntax and basic programming concepts evolved into a profound understanding of patience, persistence, and the beauty of systematic thinking. Looking back at my journey through this course, I can see the transformation not just in my technical skills, but in my mindset as an engineer and a thinker.

Learning to Code Properly

One of the most valuable lessons I learned was that "coding properly" isn't just about making something work—it's about writing code that is readable, maintainable, and elegant. Early on, I would often write solutions that technically functioned but were messy and inefficient. Through assignments and feedback, I learned the importance of clean code structure, meaningful variable names, proper commenting, and following established conventions. I discovered that code is read far more often than it is written, and poor code creates technical debt that haunts future work.

I also learned the discipline of designing solutions before diving into implementation. Sketching out logic, understanding edge cases, and planning the architecture prevents countless hours of debugging later. This shift from "just code it" to "think it through first" has made me a significantly more effective programmer. I now approach problems systematically: understand the requirements, design the solution, implement carefully, and test thoroughly.

Embracing the Debug Mindset

If there's one skill that defines this course, it's the ability to persevere through coding blocks. Early in the year, encountering a bug would frustrate me—I'd stare at code for hours feeling stuck. But gradually, I learned that debugging is not a sign of failure; it's the actual work of programming. Every professional programmer spends more time debugging than writing new code. This realization was liberating. I learned to approach bugs with curiosity rather than frustration, asking systematically: What did I expect? What actually happened? Why is there a difference?

I developed debugging strategies that actually work: reading error messages carefully instead of dismissing them, using print statements to trace execution, breaking problems into smaller parts, writing test cases to isolate issues, and occasionally just stepping away and returning with fresh eyes. I learned that patience isn't a weakness—it's a superpower in programming. Some of my best solutions came after hours of testing and experimentation, trying different approaches until finding one that was elegant and efficient.

The Power of Testing and Experimentation

Throughout this course, I learned that computer science fundamentally requires a mindset of testing and experimentation. Whether implementing a game with collision detection, designing a sorting algorithm, or building a complex data system, success came from rigorous testing. I'd test normal cases, edge cases, boundary conditions, and error cases. This habit revealed bugs I never would have found otherwise and taught me that untested code is essentially assumed to be broken.

I also learned that experimentation is crucial for growth. Many of my best implementations came from trying different approaches, seeing what worked and what didn't, and iterating. This is especially true in game development, where game feel and user experience require constant tweaking and feedback loops. In data analysis, I experimented with different algorithms and data structures to optimize performance. In system design, I prototyped multiple architectures to find the most elegant solution. Experimentation isn't inefficient—it's essential.

Technical Growth and Understanding Systems

Beyond coding basics, I've developed a much deeper understanding of how systems work. I went from thinking of programs as isolated scripts to understanding how data flows through systems, how algorithms trade off speed for space, how different approaches scale to massive data volumes. I learned about algorithm complexity, Big O notation, and why seemingly small changes in implementation can cause orders-of-magnitude differences in performance.

I also developed systems thinking—the ability to visualize how components interact, anticipate cascading effects of changes, and design architectures that are both powerful and maintainable. Working on larger projects forced me to think about separation of concerns, modularity, and interfaces between components. I learned that elegant system design reduces bugs and makes future development easier.

Connection to My Engineering Goals

This course has solidified my commitment to engineering. I now understand that engineering is ultimately about solving problems within constraints—constraints of physics, resources, time, and requirements. Programming is applied problem-solving at its core, and mastering it gives me tools I'll need across every engineering discipline. Whether I'm designing medical devices, building systems, or solving scientific problems, the debugging mindset, systematic thinking, and testing discipline I've developed will be essential.

I also see how computer science intersects with my interests in medicine and science. Modern medicine and research are increasingly computational. Understanding systems, writing efficient algorithms, and designing robust software could directly enable me to contribute to healthcare innovation and scientific discovery.

Looking Forward

As I move forward, I'm committed to deepening my expertise in complex coding systems. I want to study data structures more deeply, understand concurrency and distributed systems, and master modern frameworks and paradigms. But more importantly, I'm committed to maintaining the mindset I've developed: patient, systematic, testing-oriented, and growth-focused. I've learned that becoming an excellent engineer isn't about raw talent—it's about discipline, curiosity, and willingness to persist through challenges.

The bomb-finding game I built in this course is a simple example, but it represents this transformation. It required understanding game state, implementing collision detection, managing timing, and iteratively improving gameplay. The projects I've built—in data analysis, algorithm visualization, and system monitoring—all taught me that complex systems are just combinations of well-understood components, carefully orchestrated and thoroughly tested.

Computer science has taught me that there's always a solution to any problem if you approach it systematically, test it rigorously, and persist when facing obstacles. That's not just a valuable lesson for programming—it's a lesson for life and engineering.
